4:1 and Counting: Why Single Women Vastly Outnumber Men on the Mission Field

A Forum Talk for Warm Beach Camp
Noah Stone

Section 1: Challenges Single Women Face on the Mission Field

Before we examine why so few young men are going, we must first honor the many faithful single women who have already answered the call. Their sacrifice is real and costly. Major evangelical mission agencies confirm the imbalance is long-standing and most acute in the hardest places.

Documented Realities

  • Pioneers reports that “about two-thirds of missionaries are married couples, and 70–80% of the rest are single women” (Wade 2021).
  • Crossworld states: “Single missionary women outnumber single missionary men four to one” and asks, “With nearly two-thirds of missionaries being women, what picture does this paint…? Does it look like Christianity is for women but not for men?” (Jenney 2023).
  • OMF notes the strategic mismatch: “Roughly 25 percent of the world’s unreached people are single men, while roughly 1 percent of the global missions’ force is single men” (Hawke 2019).
  • In high-risk areas, the ratio has been reported as high as seven single women to one single man (Ripken 2023). My own decade of observation among Central Asian Muslim peoples aligns with the upper end of these estimates—often five or more single women for every single man.

Core Challenges Single Women Face

  1. Desire for Partnership — Many single women deeply desire a godly husband who shares their calling, yet qualified single men are rare on the field.

  2. Loneliness and Isolation — Cultural and security barriers in closed or difficult countries make deep friendships hard to form and sustain.

  3. Cultural and Safety Pressures — In many host societies, single women face suspicion, restricted movement, and constant need for extra wisdom and protection.

  4. Heavier Spiritual and Emotional Loads — When couples leave the field early, single women often absorb greater responsibility for sustaining the work and discipling others (Ripken 2023).

  5. Influence Hierarchy on Teams — Practitioner reports describe a de facto order of influence: married men, single men, married women, single women—with single women frequently feeling like an afterthought (Ralston 2021).

  6. Grieving Diminished Marriage Prospects — The mathematical reality of the ratio forces many women to confront the possibility of lifelong singleness earlier and more acutely than they expected (Hartz 2024; TEAM 2017).

  7. Attrition and Re-entry Strain — Single women show higher burnout risk and more difficult re-entry transitions without a spouse or family support network.

The Gift of Singleness for Frontier Ministry

Scripture speaks directly to the strategic value of singleness for the sake of the gospel:

“I wish that all were as I myself am. But each has his own gift from God, one of one kind and one of another. To the unmarried and the widows I say that it is good for them to remain single as I am.” (1 Corinthians 7:7–8)

“The unmarried man is anxious about the things of the Lord, how to please the Lord. But the married man is anxious about worldly things, how to please his wife, and his interests are divided. … I say this for your own benefit, not to lay any restraint upon you, but to promote good order and to secure your undivided devotion to the Lord.” (1 Corinthians 7:32–35)

Jesus said, “There are eunuchs who have made themselves eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven. Let the one who is able to receive this receive it.” (Matthew 19:12)

Singleness is not a waiting room. It is a strategic gift that grants greater freedom to go to hard places, move quickly, take risks, and give undivided attention to the work—precisely the realities required in frontier and creative-access contexts.

Transition to Testimony

These realities are not theoretical. I want to invite you now to hear a testimony of your sister in Christ who has lived them.

Section 2: Why Young Men Are Not Going into Missions

We have celebrated the women who are going. Now we must honestly ask: Where are the young men? Why is the ratio so unbalanced?

Documented Realities

  • IMB President Paul Chitwood stated officially: “Single women outnumber single men serving through the IMB by two-to-one” (Chitwood 2022).
  • The best available survey (Missio Nexus 2021) found 74% of single long-term missionaries are female—approximately 2.85:1 (Zurlo 2025).
  • The conventional figure across evangelical faith missions remains 70–80% of singles are women, or roughly 4:1 (Wade 2021; Jenney 2023; Akin 2022).

Seven Recurring Barriers for Young Men

  1. The Pastoral-Track Siphon — In complementarian settings, devout young men are often steered toward seminary and domestic pulpits—vocations of status and salary closed to women. Missions becomes the primary outlet for zealous women but not for men (Wade 2021; Akin 2022; Ripken 2023).

  2. Provider-Role Economics and Support Raising — Men who feel responsible to provide for a future family struggle with the uncertainty of raised support. “As men, we are expected to be the provider, and a lot of guys find it very difficult to ask for money and live full time on support” (Café 1040; Piper 2016; Jenney 2023).

  3. Student Debt — Average college debt of ~$30,000 collides with agency policies against sending candidates with significant debt (Akin 2022).

  4. Marriage-First Sequencing — Many young men will not go unless they first find a wife willing to go. “The very things that keep a man single in his late 20s and 30s are probably the same kind of things that would keep him from pursuing a life of missions” (Piper 2016; Jenney 2023).

  5. Spiritual Immaturity and Pornography — High rates of pornography use among young Christian men create shame, disqualification, and relational brokenness. One analysis found 77% of Christian men ages 18–30 view pornography at least monthly (Jenney 2023; Akin 2022).

  6. Parental Discouragement — Even believing parents often subtly or overtly discourage sons from going overseas, preferring safety, proximity, and financial security. Barna research found only 35% of engaged Christian parents would definitely encourage their child to serve in missions (Barna Group 2020, via Medina 2025; Jenney 2023).

  7. Risk Aversion and Comfort — Missions is frequently framed as a short-term trip rather than a lifelong vocation. The idol of safety and success has replaced a willingness to suffer for the gospel (Jenney 2023; Buser 2019).

Paul Akin summarizes the root issue with clarity:

“I’m convinced the lack of young men on the mission field is first and foremost a local church issue. Older men in the church can purposefully disciple young men with the nations in mind… show the next generation that God is a missionary God and the Bible is a missionary book.” (Akin 2022)

A counter-datum worth noting: Barna research indicates that actively churched young men express more interest in missions as a career than young women. The bottleneck is not raw interest but the pipeline between aspiration and deployment.

Section 3: Where the Church Must Take Ownership (ACCN)

This is not only a young-men problem or a young-women problem. The church has to take ownership. The agencies themselves locate primary responsibility in the local church. As Paul Akin writes, it is “first and foremost a local church issue.”

For the Apostolic Christian Church of the Nazarene, we have a beautiful heritage of practical service and community care. Yet if we want to see more of our young people—especially young men and young couples—respond to the Great Commission in its fullness, we must address several structural gaps.

A Word on Urgency

The clock is not neutral. Back in 2015, SEND International’s Chandelle Body, reflecting on Missions Fest Seattle, estimated that roughly 75 percent of the people in the room were over fifty, with “more than half probably over 65,” and she asked the question that should sober all of us: “in 20-30 years … who on earth will be supporting missions?” (Body 2015). Look around the next missions gathering you attend—look around a room like this one—and you may well see the same thing. And notice that her concern cuts both ways: it is not only about who will go, but about who will pray, give, and send. If we do not raise up the next generation now, the sending base and the sending force will grow gray and thin together.

Eight Areas Where the Church Must Take Ownership

  1. Lack of Partnership with Mission Organizations
    ACCN missions activity tends to flow through its own affiliated projects rather than broad, strategic partnerships with established evangelical sending agencies focused on unreached peoples. This limits options for members called to frontier work.

  2. Lack of a Clear Pipeline or Process in Churches
    There is no standardized, denomination-wide pathway for identifying, discipling, testing, training, and sending potential missionaries. Calling often remains individual or dependent on local enthusiasm rather than a deliberate process.

  3. Lack of Local Church Polity to Support Missionaries
    Support for missionaries is heavily dependent on personal networks and ad-hoc efforts rather than clear, agreed-upon policies for financial, prayer, and member-care support once workers are sent.

  4. Lack of Denominational Structures for Non-Traditional Workers
    Members serving through other organizations—or in creative-access roles—often lack clear denominational backing, financial channels, or ongoing care structures.

  5. Lack of Trips to See Ministry in Hard Places
    Exposure to cross-cultural ministry, especially in challenging or unreached contexts, appears limited. Most service opportunities lean toward more accessible or humanitarian-focused projects.

  6. Strategic Gap Between Current Work and Unreached Frontiers
    I have not personally visited our international works, but my father-in-law has. The efforts in South America (Brazil, Paraguay, Argentina) and Zambia (Dennis Delic) are often strongly evangelical, with leaders sharing the gospel and planting churches. These are good and fruitful. However, these contexts already have a high concentration of Christians. The greater strategic need lies in North Africa, the Middle East, and Central Asia—predominantly Islamic regions with very little Christian witness of any kind. Our current emphasis on humanitarian work in more accessible, Christian-majority areas leaves a gap in reaching those with no gospel access.

  7. Lack of Preaching, Discipleship, and Parental Encouragement
    Our pulpits rarely set the frontier before young men. Older men are not systematically discipling the next generation “with the nations in mind.” Parents— even believing parents—often discourage sons from going overseas. We must recover a culture in which preaching, discipleship, and parental blessing actively encourage and commission young men and young couples for the hardest places.

  8. Under-Utilization of Non-Traditional and Professional Pathways
    Full-time church planters and evangelists are still desperately needed. At the same time, the remaining hard-to-reach places require doctors, engineers, academics, agricultural specialists, linguists, business people, and other professionals who can gain long-term access. This style of ministry aligns well with our historic strength in bivocational service. The Lausanne Movement’s Business as Mission Manifesto calls churches to “identify, affirm, pray for, commission and release business people and entrepreneurs… among all peoples and to the ends of the earth” (Lausanne Movement 2004). These pathways can also help address male-specific barriers around career continuity and provision. We must value both.

Conclusion: The Whole Church Responding

The shortage of young men and young couples is not a problem women can fix by themselves, and it is not a problem men can fix by themselves. It requires the whole body—brothers and sisters together—to repent where needed, pray fervently, and act with courage.

For the women among us: Thank you for your faithfulness. Continue to serve with excellence. Pray for and encourage the men in your lives—brothers, friends, potential spouses—toward maturity and bold obedience. Guard against lowering standards or pursuing relationships that would pull a man away from a clear calling.

For the men among us: The harvest is plentiful, but the workers—especially male workers—are few. The barriers are real, but they are not insurmountable. Older men: disciple younger men with the nations in mind. Younger men: count the cost, repent of comfort and pornography where needed, and be willing to go—or to send others at great cost.

For the whole church (ACCN): We have a heritage of caring for people in practical ways. Now let us also raise up, train, and send laborers—especially young men and young couples—to the places where the gospel has not yet gone. Let us build clear pipelines, form strategic partnerships, value both humanitarian service and bold gospel proclamation, and use every legitimate means (including professional pathways) to reach those who have never heard.

The harvest is still plentiful. God is still calling people. May we be found faithful.
