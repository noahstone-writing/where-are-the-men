"We Don't Need Fewer Women; We Need More Men": Evangelical Mission Agencies and the Gender Imbalance in the Contemporary Missionary Force

Noah Stone

Abstract

Single women have outnumbered single men in the Protestant missionary force for more than a century, yet the phenomenon has received little systematic scholarly attention and, until recently, little candid discussion from the sending agencies themselves. This article surveys the current public position of the major evangelical sending agencies. These are the International Mission Board (IMB), Pioneers, OMF International, Crossworld, ABWE, SEND International, SIM, TEAM, Wycliffe Bible Translators, Frontiers, and Cru. In official statements and agency-platformed media, they speak to the male-to-female ratio among missionaries, the causes of the male deficit, the burdens the imbalance places on single women, and the remedies proposed. Documentary analysis of agency publications, denominational reporting, parachurch commentary, and survey data is supplemented by the author's participant observation across ten years of service among Central Asian Muslim peoples with three agencies. The analysis yields four principal findings. First, defensible statistics range from 2:1 (IMB, the only president-level official figure) through 2.85:1 (Missio Nexus 2021 survey data) to the conventional 4:1, a figure best characterized as widespread estimate rather than census fact. Second, agencies attribute the deficit primarily to church-culture causes (the domestic pastoral track, provider-role economics, student debt, and discipleship failure) rather than to recruitment practices. Third, prescriptive literature locates responsibility overwhelmingly in the local church. Fourth, although professional and marketplace pathways are promoted as strategic access to restricted countries, no agency explicitly connects these pathways to the recruitment of men; that connection, while plausible, remains an inference. The article closes with implications for congregations and denominations whose international engagement is currently weighted toward humanitarian work.

Keywords: missionary demographics, gender, single missionaries, mission agencies, mobilization, tentmaking, business as mission, local church, Central Asia, participant observation

1. Introduction

In 1911, Samuel Zwemer asked whether there was "a more heroic test for the powers of manhood than pioneer work in the mission field" (quoted in Akin 2022). More than a century later, an article on the mission agency Crossworld's website put the question more bluntly: "Men on Mission: Where Are They?" (Jenney 2023). The century between them changed little about the pattern both texts name. Women have outnumbered men in the Protestant missionary force since at least 1900 (Robert 1997; Wade 2021), and among unmarried missionaries the gap is stark: current estimates of the ratio of single women to single men run from two-to-one to more than four-to-one, with the highest figures reported for exactly those fields (the "unreached" and "creative-access" contexts) where evangelical agencies say the need is greatest.

Evangelical missions circles acknowledge the pattern openly and lament it often. Rigorous examination is rare. Gina Zurlo, co-director of the Center for the Study of Global Christianity, puts the American missionary movement at roughly two-thirds female (most of those women young and single), while noting that hard data on the gender composition of the force largely do not exist (Zurlo 2025). John Piper, asked why women are the more eager missionaries, answered with unusual candor for the genre: "I think the most honest answer would be: I don't know. And if the research has been done to get the answers, I am not aware of it" (Piper 2016).

This article does not claim to settle the causal question empirically; no dataset assembled here could. Its primary aim is descriptive and documentary: to establish, from primary sources, what the major evangelical sending agencies themselves currently say about the gender imbalance. Description, however, does not leave causation untouched. In gathering and synthesizing the explanations the agencies and their closely associated voices offer (§5), the article assembles a composite account of why so few young men go. That account remains informed conjecture rather than tested finding, and I hold it as such throughout; but it is a substantive contribution in its own right, and, I will argue, among the more consequential things this literature has to say. Four research questions structure the inquiry:

  1. What statistics or official statements do agencies publish concerning the male-to-female ratio among single missionaries, and concerning the presence of young couples?
  2. What explanations do agencies and closely associated voices offer for the scarcity of young men in long-term cross-cultural service?
  3. What consequences of the imbalance do these sources identify, particularly for single women on the field?
  4. What remedies are proposed or implemented (including the promotion of professional, "tentmaking," and marketplace pathways), and how is responsibility distributed between agency and local church?

The question matters beyond demography. If, as Crossworld's Joel Jenney argues, a two-thirds-female missionary force risks signaling to unreached populations that "Christianity is for women but not for men" (Jenney 2023), and if, as OMF's John Hawke calculates, single men make up roughly a quarter of the world's unreached people but roughly one percent of the missionary force (Hawke 2019), then the composition of the sending body is itself a missiological variable, not merely an administrative one.

2. Sources and Method

The study proceeds by documentary analysis of publicly available material from the eleven agencies named above, with denominational press, parachurch commentary, survey reports, and academic missiology added in. Searches ran in mid-2026 across agency websites, the Missio Nexus corpus, Evangelical Missions Quarterly, denominational news services, and the major evangelical platforms (The Gospel Coalition, Desiring God, Radical, Outreach Magazine). Claims central to the argument were cross-checked independently. Two circulating claims failed that check; both are excluded or corrected below (§3, §5).

A word on positionality. I write not only as a researcher but as a participant. For ten years I have served among Central Asian peoples, in mostly Muslim contexts, under three sending organizations. Two were large agencies, each numbering in the thousands of members; the third was much smaller, numbering some hundreds. Where it bears on the argument, I draw on that experience as participant observation (§3.5, §5, §6), marked as such and held to the usual limits of the genre: my observations are regional, unaudited, and dependent on memory, and I leave the organizations unnamed for field-security reasons. I offer them not as a substitute for roster data (which, as this article documents, the agencies do not publish) but as one worker's ground-level check on the estimates that circulate in their absence.

Because the evidence base is mixed, the article keeps four tiers of source authority apart:

  1. Official agency statements are texts by agency principals or formally adopted communications (e.g., IMB President Paul Chitwood's first-person articles; SIM's official question-and-answer features).
  2. Staff-authored content on official agency platforms, that is, articles by agency writers or field workers published on agency websites (e.g., Wade 2021 for Pioneers; Parrish 2018 for IMB; Jenney 2023 for Crossworld). These carry institutional weight without being adopted policy.
  3. Agency-platformed media are podcasts and interviews that agencies host but that feature outside guests (e.g., ABWE's Missions Podcast), whose views should not be ascribed to the agency.
  4. Individual and parachurch commentary, meaning writing by agency-connected individuals on third-party platforms (Akin 2022; Buser 2019; Piper 2016) and practitioner blogs (Hartz 2024; Ralston 2021).

Three limitations deserve notice at the outset. First, several agency websites block automated retrieval; quotations first recovered through search-index extraction were re-verified in an independent check (July 2026) against live pages, archived snapshots, or primary documents, which confirmed nearly all wording verbatim. The few claims I could not confirm were corrected or removed, and the remaining caveats are listed in the notes.¹ Second, agencies rarely publish roster-level demographic data, so the statistical picture (§3) rests largely on self-reported survey aggregates and conventional estimates. Third, the corpus is overwhelmingly North American; the growing majority-world missionary movement, whose gender dynamics may differ (cf. Zurlo 2025), lies beyond this article's scope.

3. The Statistical Landscape: What Can Be Defended

3.1 The conventional figures

One set of figures turns up again and again across agency and parachurch writing, and it barely shifts from source to source. Pioneers puts it plainly, noting that "about two-thirds of missionaries are married couples, and 70-80% of the rest are single women" (Wade 2021). Crossworld sharpens the ratio: "Single missionary women outnumber single missionary men four to one, meaning more than 80% of all single missionaries are women" (Jenney 2023). Africa Inland Mission, reporting through Mission Network News, set the single-worker figure at "for AIM and other similar organizations, somewhere between 80 and 85 percent of all single missionaries are women" (Mission Network News 2013). Piper (2016) breaks the force down the same way (one-third married men, one-third married women, and roughly 80 percent of the remaining third single women), and lands on "something just less than two-thirds of the total missionary force are women." At the far edge sits Nik Ripken, a veteran of the IMB, who claims a seven-to-one disparity for the mission field generally, adding that where safety is threatened the ratio climbs higher still (Ripken 2023). The specific seven-to-one figure turns up nowhere else in the corpus, and I treat that number as uncorroborated. Ripken's vantage point, however, is not incidental to it. "Nik Ripken" is itself a pseudonym, adopted because his working life was spent among peoples and in places hostile to the Christian faith, and it is precisely those frontier and pioneer contexts—where every source in this section agrees the imbalance is sharpest—that he knows at first hand. His sense of the ratio in such settings therefore carries a weight that its lack of external corroboration should not be permitted to erase: he is reporting from the very end of the distribution this article is most concerned to characterize, and where published data are scarcest for reasons taken up in §3.3.

3.2 The provenance problem

The figures agree with each other. That agreement proves less than it looks, though, because they draw on one shared pool of estimates rather than on separate measurements. The Gospel Coalition's main treatment is candid about exactly this: "Widespread anecdotal data suggests that women outnumber men on the mission field by a ratio of at least 2:1" (Akin 2022, emphasis added). Zurlo, writing from inside quantitative missiology, reads the two-thirds-female picture as a widespread estimate with no rigorous footing, and notes that the agencies which track gender at all tend to track it only for their single members (Zurlo 2025). One point has to be made against a stronger claim sometimes met in this literature. The data are not wholly absent, and the assertion that the ratio "cannot be verified statistically" does not survive the evidence set out below. But the popular 4:1 figure is best read as the high end of a conventional range, not a census fact.

The pool also has a traceable headwater. The earliest empirical anchor I can locate for the 80–85 percent band is Howard Erickson's "Single Missionary Survey" in the February 1989 Fundamentalist Journal: nineteen agencies, 20,333 missionaries, over 16 percent of them (3,320) unmarried; among the singles only 15 percent were men—which is to say, 85 percent of singles were women, or in the survey's own words "a ratio of one single male missionary to every six single female missionaries" (Erickson 1989). The survey was cited in Recovering Biblical Manhood and Womanhood (Piper and Grudem 1991), a plausible vector into the evangelical mainstream—though the footnote there miscites the issue (January for February), an error each later borrowing had no way to catch—and its band matches exactly the figure AIM reported through Mission Network News in 2013 and the one Piper repeated, without a source, in 2016. The conventional figure, in other words, is less a floating estimate than a fossil: a survey result now thirty-seven years old, repeated as if current. And setting it beside the best modern aggregate (§3.3) yields a finding I have not seen stated anywhere in this literature: on the only two large-scale measurements available, the singles imbalance has narrowed, from 85 percent female in 1989 to 74 percent in 2021.

3.3 The firmest data points

Three data points stand out, and they stand out because of where they come from.

First, the only president-level official agency statistic I could locate anywhere in the corpus. Writing in his official capacity in June 2022, IMB President Paul Chitwood stated, "Single women outnumber single men serving through the IMB by two-to-one" (Chitwood 2022). The largest denominational agency's own internal ratio (2:1) sits well below the conventional 4:1.

Second, the best survey aggregate on offer. As Zurlo reports it, Missio Nexus's 2021 data collection counted 36,323 long-term missionaries (defined as serving at least two years) across reporting member agencies, of whom 53 percent were women overall; among single missionaries, 74 percent were women against 26 percent men (Zurlo 2025; cf. Missio Nexus 2021). Seventy-four percent works out to a ratio of roughly 2.85:1, again below the conventional figure.

Third, a rare agency-specific roster description. David Smith, a worker with WEC International, called his agency "about sixty-two percent female," made up of roughly 31 percent single women and 7 percent single men (Ask A Missionary n.d.), about 4.4:1, the closest thing here to roster-level confirmation of the conventional figure, though it comes from an individual rather than an official source.

That the firmest numbers should surface this way—a president writing about abuse prevention, a survey aggregate, an individual worker, and (below, §3.5) a director speaking privately—is not accidental, and the pattern deserves to be named rather than merely lamented. Agencies are deeply reluctant to publish roster-level data on personnel serving in secure or creative-access locations, and for sound reason: a public breakdown of who serves where, in what numbers, and in what family configuration can expose missionaries and, more dangerously, their local partners to surveillance, harassment, and reprisal. The statistical vacuum this article repeatedly runs into is therefore not simply administrative negligence or indifference to research; it is in part a deliberate security posture, and it is most pronounced in exactly the frontier contexts where the imbalance is sharpest and the data would be most valuable. Much of what can be said quantitatively must accordingly be reconstructed from the partial, the aggregated, and the anonymized.

So the defensible summary points in a direction rather than to a precise number. The estimates run from 2:1 (official, IMB) through 2.85:1 (survey) to 4:1 and above (agency convention), and they all point the same way; the imbalance is sharpest, on the agencies' own account, "especially in the most challenging places" (Wade 2021).

3.4 Historical depth

None of this is new. Pioneers again: "Since 1900, missionary women have outnumbered missionary men, sometimes by a 2:1 ratio," and, Wade adds, "especially in the most challenging places" (Wade 2021); Dana Robert's standard history reaches the same two-to-one reading for twentieth-century faith missions (Robert 1997). Wycliffe's own institutional record hands us some striking early numbers. Among Summer Institute of Linguistics workers in Mexico, "in early 1941, 22 out of 37 linguists working in Mexico were women," and "by the fall of 1944, two-thirds of 122 members were women" (Wycliffe Bible Translators USA c. 2020). OMF International's predecessor, the China Inland Mission, built the pattern into its structure back in the nineteenth century: Hudson Taylor called women "the most powerful agency" for reaching Chinese homes, and by 1882 the CIM roster listed 56 wives and 95 single women (Christianity Today 2023; OMF International n.d.-a). The imbalance runs through the whole modern history of the faith-missions movement. It is a structural feature, not a recent turn.

3.5 Participant observation from Central Asia

To that documentary record I want to add what I have seen myself, across ten years among Central Asian Muslim peoples and three organizations. It is anecdotal, in the strict sense of the word. But it bears straight on the question I raised in §3.3 (whether the frontier ratio runs higher than the survey aggregates), and it comes from exactly the kind of place Pioneers has in mind, the "most challenging places" where the imbalance is said to bite hardest (Wade 2021).

In the two large agencies I have served with (each of them ran to thousands of members), what I saw, year after year, was single women outnumbering single men by at least five to one. In my own country of service with one of the two, I cannot bring to mind a single unmarried man among the workers I knew, set against at least five single women. That impression needs an honest qualification. I serve in a secure context, and my exposure to the wider organization—its other teams, regions, and platforms—is limited; I do not doubt that single men serve elsewhere within it, but I hold no roster data, and my recollection speaks only to the portion of the organization I could actually see. What I offer, then, is the shape of my own field of view, not a census of the agency. The smaller organization lets me get closer to an actual count. Its international director shared his membership figures with me personally, and they resolve like this: single workers made up roughly 15 percent of the organization's total personnel (about one member in seven), and the other 85 percent were married. Inside that single minority, single women outnumbered single men by more than four to one, and plausibly five to one. Set against the whole force, single men came to less than 3 percent of personnel; single women, roughly 12 percent. That is, alongside Chitwood (2022), one of the very few points in this study where an agency leader actually puts a number on the imbalance, even if he did it privately. The proportions are worth holding up against the published comparators. The single-male share (under 3 percent) sits below even the 7 percent reported for WEC International (§3.3), and it leans toward Hawke's global estimate that single men make up roughly 1 percent of the missionary force (Hawke 2019); the singles share overall (about 15 percent) runs a little below SIM's self-reported 20 percent (SIM n.d.). And in the large agency I serve with now, I have yet to meet a single man who stayed single. I have known three. All three married. On my present team there are seven single women and not one single man.

Three things follow from this. First, what I watched on the ground in a Muslim-majority frontier context lines up with the top of the published range, nearer 5:1 than the survey aggregate's 2.85:1. That gives qualified support to the claim that the imbalance gets worse as the field gets harder, and it makes Ripken's otherwise uncorroborated seven-to-one figure (Ripken 2023) look less far-fetched than it does standing on its own. Second, the imbalance does not just sit there; it is remade by a process you can only see over time. In my experience the single men who do come tend to marry and not come back, so the category "single male missionary" keeps emptying itself even when it briefly fills. Third, female singleness on the field has an attrition shape of its own. Single women in my context have struggled to get past the three-year mark abroad; those who do get past it tend to stay much longer. That bimodal pattern muddies any flat comparison of male and female persistence, and it finds a later echo in the age-forty decision point that OMF's singles research picks out for single women (§6).

3.6 Young couples: an evidentiary gap

On the second demographic worry, the shortage of young couples, the documentary record is thin. No agency I surveyed publishes an age distribution for its missionaries, or any figure that isolates couples under thirty-five. What evidence there is comes at the question sideways. The Evangelical Free Church of America's ReachGlobal reports that "since 2017, the number of young people applying to serve with ReachGlobal has steadily declined," that "the average age of ReachGlobal missionaries has steadily increased," and that "in the next five years nearly one-third of ReachGlobal's staff will retire" (Medina 2025). The Lausanne Movement's North American analysis warns of "an impending 'Great Retirement' of Boomers beginning in 2030" (Lausanne Movement n.d.). Married couples make up roughly two-thirds of the force on every accounting, so a sustained drop in young applicants must, arithmetically, mean a drop in young couples too. But that specific claim still waits on direct measurement, and researchers should resist the unattributed "average missionary age" figures floating around on data-aggregation websites, none of which come with a traceable method.

4. Agency Voices: A Survey

4.1 Agencies that address the imbalance directly

Crossworld gives the corpus its most direct treatment. Jenney's article supplies the canonical 4:1 statistic and frames the deficit as a witness problem, asking, "With nearly two-thirds of missionaries being women, what picture does this paint for the local people...? Does it look like Christianity is for women but not for men?" It then delivers the corpus's most-quoted line: "We don't need fewer women; we need more men!" (Jenney 2023). A companion field-side piece by a Crossworld worker in East Asia, "Mind the Man Gap," reports the practical upshot. No single men are on hand to disciple young local men. The church, it argues, has "idolized marriage and demonized singleness" (Crossworld 2017).

Pioneers offers the most analytically ambitious agency treatment. Wade (2021) puts forward four explanations: women's greater religious engagement in general; the vocational channel ("If a man's faith is on fire and he wants to serve the Lord, he's more likely to head to seminary and become a pastor. However, if you're female, those doors may not be open to you"); the different economics of provision for men and women; and women's strategic indispensability for reaching secluded women. The tone stays explanatory and appreciative, not alarmed.

OMF International contributes the most striking strategic datum. Hawke (2019), writing for OMF (U.S.), observes: "Roughly 25 percent of the world's unreached people are single men. Roughly 1 percent of the global missions' force is single men." Hawke gives the figures without a source, but the mismatch they describe is missiologically pointed: in many East Asian contexts single local men are among the hardest people to reach, and a single male missionary is a natural bridge to them. OMF has put work into singleness resources too. Its guide Living and Serving as a Single Missionary draws on a study of 120 missionaries and marks age forty as a turning point, the moment when many single women make hard decisions about their future in missions, and it runs alongside related podcast programming (OMF International n.d.-b). Its German branch supplies something found nowhere else in this corpus: the only non-Anglophone agency treatment of the male shortage, a 2023 episode of OMF Deutschland's Mango Radio podcast titled "Männermangel in der Mission: Mann, mach was!" ("Male shortage in missions: man, do something!"), which frames men pragmatically as needed so that other men can hear the gospel (OMF Deutschland 2023). The register is worth marking: it mobilizes without the masculinity-crisis frame (§4.4), and it stands against a German evangelical landscape that otherwise does not discuss the imbalance at all—Evangelium21, the German Reformed network that translates Gospel Coalition and Desiring God content weekly, has published nothing on it, and the oldest German institutional response to the female surplus, the Frauen-Missions-Gebetsbund (founded 1900), has organized gratitude for single women missionaries, not alarm about missing men, for a century and a quarter.

IMB presents a two-level picture. Its official statistic (Chitwood 2022) has already been noted. Its main published treatment pulls the other way. Parrish's "The Gender Divide in Missions" (2018), written by an IMB staff writer, is affirmative rather than alarmed. It narrates women's role in missions "beginning with an absolute aversion to sending females, to a tentative acceptance of their presence overseas, to a vibrant and growing appreciation for their insight and contributions," and it insists that "the call of God to make his glory known and his name great is the call for both genders, and neither can fulfill that call without the other." It closes on the same note. "Single or married, male or female," Parrish writes, "all are needed on the mission field." Official IMB mobilization rhetoric under both David Platt and Chitwood stays consistently gender-neutral ("multitudes more men and women"; "call out the called"; "We're not discharging soldiers... We're calling in reinforcements" [Chitwood, quoted in Baptist Courier 2021]). The "more men" alarm in the Southern Baptist orbit comes instead from IMB-connected individuals on other platforms: Paul Akin (Platt's former senior aide, now provost of Southern Seminary) at The Gospel Coalition (Akin 2022), and Brooks Buser of Radius International on Platt's Radical platform (Buser 2019). Meanwhile the IMB runs a whole content vertical aimed at single women, with no male counterpart. It offers preparation guides, care articles, and commemorations of Lottie Moon, Martha Myers, and Mavis Pate (IMB 2020). Its flagship annual offering carries a single woman's name too. That asymmetry of address is itself evidence of where the demographic weight lies.

ABWE takes up the question through its media platform rather than through official statements, and it frames things its own way. Its Missions Podcast gave a 2019 episode to "Why are there so few men in missions?", in which the guest Michael Foster put the shortage down to "the overall downgrade of teaching on biblical manhood in evangelicalism" (ABWE International 2019); a 2026 successor episode argued that evangelical culture had gone "feminine coded" (ABWE International 2026). These are guest positions on agency-hosted media, not agency policy, and they carry in a contested masculinity discourse that I examine further in §5.

4.2 Agencies that address singleness pastorally but not the ratio

A second group of agencies publishes a good deal on singleness in missions while staying silent on the gender ratio itself.

SIM counts its single members. As the agency puts it, "Currently, SIM has 4,000 workers around the world. Among these, there are about 700 who are single. That translates to roughly 20 per cent of our people" (SIM n.d.). It publishes no gender breakdown of those seven hundred, which is itself telling. Its singleness content leans toward member care ("we don't put people in places by themselves"), and it gives space to a rare single-male testimony, the African worker Francis Kuntenga: "I believe churches should stop pushing people into marriage simply because they are gifted in ministry.... Being single has opened doors. I have worked in places like the DRC and northern Nigeria, where I could not have taken a wife or family" (SIM 2025).

TEAM's feature "What It's Like to Be a Single Missionary" (first published 2017, updated 2025) records both sides. There is the access advantage of singleness (a village "can manage" hosting one person where a family would overwhelm it), and there are the costs, among them the marriage-hope grief of a worker in Chad: "At age 39, I was pretty sure it wouldn't happen, so then I started to spiral out of control" (TEAM 2017). Tellingly, when TEAM's official explainer on member care needed a stock case, it reached for "a single woman in a male-dominated culture... limited in where she can go and what she can do" (Watson 2014). TEAM's one contribution on young couples is cautionary: most agencies want at least a year of marriage before deployment, and newlyweds get counseled to build the marriage before they go to the field (Mills 2016).

SEND International yields something no other agency does, an official statement on gender from its chief executive, though aimed at leadership rather than at the field ratio. Michelle Atwell, on being appointed to lead SEND US in 2017, said: "If women have made such fabulous contributions on the field, why not in the boardroom or in the corner office? The Great Commission doesn't apply just to men, it applies to all of us" (SEND International 2017). Atwell there names the paradox that frames this whole literature: a female-majority field force under male-majority governance. SEND also publishes worry about the aging of the missions constituency. Reflecting on Missions Fest Seattle, SEND's Chandelle Body estimated that roughly three-quarters of those in the room were over fifty, "more than half probably over 65," and asked, "in 20-30 years … who on earth will be supporting missions?" (Body 2015). Her worry is pointedly double-edged, aimed as much at the aging of missions' supporters as of its goers; as an individual staff member's eyeball estimate at a single conference, it is best read as vivid illustration rather than data, but it converges with the firmer aging-workforce evidence taken up in §3.6. It runs a formal Women's Ministry with no male counterpart.

Wycliffe Bible Translators handles gender composition only through celebratory institutional history, the pioneering single-women teams of Hansen and Pike from 1936, and the 1940s workforce data cited in §3.4 (Wycliffe Bible Translators USA c. 2020). It currently runs a women-targeted engagement initiative, Women of the Word (Wycliffe Bible Translators USA n.d.), again with no male counterpart.

4.3 Documented silences

Frontiers works only among Muslim peoples, the very contexts where the imbalance is reportedly sharpest. It publishes worker reflections that own the hardships of singleness on the field (Frontiers USA 2020) and celebrates single women workers in its storytelling, but I could locate no statement on gender ratios, male recruitment, or young couples; its security-driven publishing culture, where anonymous authorship is standard, plausibly plays a part. Cru publishes a theology of singleness (Johnson n.d.) and testimony from single women on staff, but nothing public on its staff gender composition, whether statement or dataset. The only quantified picture is still external journalism from 1992, when Campus Crusade counted 791 women to 660 men on U.S. campus staff while only two of 164 campus directors were women (Christianity Today 1992). For both organizations, as for SIM, TEAM, and Wycliffe on the ratio question, the silence is itself a finding. The agencies most exposed to the imbalance do not talk about it in public, and the "where are the men" literature comes almost entirely from a handful of agencies (Crossworld, Pioneers, OMF) and from parachurch commentary.

4.4 A difference of register

A pattern runs across §§4.1–4.3, and it tracks agency type. Organizations built around unreached-peoples or frontier work (Pioneers, OMF, Crossworld, and the individuals in the IMB/Radius orbit) take up the imbalance analytically and strategically, because in restricted-access, strongly patriarchal settings the make-up of the team is what constrains the work most. Generalist agencies (SIM, TEAM, SEND, Wycliffe, Cru) treat singleness mainly as a member-care matter, and gender mainly as a celebration of what women have contributed. The masculinity-crisis register (ABWE's platform) makes a third stream. It overlaps with intra-evangelical arguments about complementarianism and the formation of men, and it should be read as contested discourse rather than as agency consensus.

5. Explaining the Male Deficit

Across the corpus, seven explanations recur. Their authors offer them as informed conjecture (Piper labels his own an "opinion, not strong conviction" (Piper 2016)), and none has been tested against data.

(1) The pastoral-track siphon. This is the most structural of the seven, and the one most peculiar to conservative evangelicalism. Devout young men in complementarian churches get routed toward seminary and the home pastorate, vocations of status and salary that stay closed to their female peers, for whom cross-cultural missions is the chief outlet for a ministry calling (Wade 2021; Akin 2022). Ripken puts the corollary bluntly: organizations "inadvertently train American men to become pastors instead of missionaries" (Ripken 2023). Read this way, the imbalance is partly a by-product of how complementarianism arranges its vocations (a point that bears directly on complementarian denominations weighing where to send). A field-side corollary compounds it: men are often expected, and sometimes required, to complete seminary or graduate school before overseas service—years their female peers, barred from the pastorate the credential serves, are not asked to spend (A Life Overseas 2022). And one further fact sets this explanation apart evidentially: it is the only one of the seven asserted from both ends of the intra-evangelical gender debate. Within days of Piper's 2016 episode, an egalitarian rebuttal located the cause in exactly this channel—gifted conservative women "may only be permitted to use those gifts on the mission field," while gifted men "are often funneled into local ministry as young men" (Beautiful Kingdom Builders 2016)—and Akin later named the identical mechanism from inside complementarianism: "biblical complementarianism rightly limits women's opportunities for ministry" at home, while "men might prefer a pastoral position at home that often pays better and seems easier" (Akin 2022). The camps disagree only over the mechanism's normative meaning (for Akin the limits are right and the remedy is to challenge men; for his critics the restriction is itself the cause), and the mechanism matches the structural account mission historians have long given of the female majority (Robert 1997; Beaver 1980; Tucker 1988)—a literature, it should be said, that the popular genre never cites.

(2) Provider-role economics. A man who takes himself to be answerable for a present or future family balks at living on raised support: for him it is "harder to raise money in a self-reliant society" (Piper 2016). One mobilization ministry lets the recruits speak for themselves: "As men, we are expected to be the provider, and a lot of guys find it very difficult to ask for money and live full time on support" (Cafe 1040 n.d.). Wade (2021) and Jenney (2023) say the same.

(3) Student debt. The money problem starts earlier still. "The average college student graduates with around $30,000 in student loan debt, and most mission agencies have policies against sending missionaries with significant debts" (Akin 2022; cf. Adams 2020).

(4) Marriage-first sequencing. Men mean to marry before they go, and they fear the field will foreclose marriage; Piper adds the reverse case: "The very things that keep a man single in his late 20s and 30s are probably the same kind of things that would keep him from pursuing a life of missions" (Piper 2016; Jenney 2023). What I saw in the field (§3.5) tells me the mechanism runs on the field too, not only before it. The few single men who do arrive tend to marry and then not come back, so that marriage works less as a door into long-term service than as a door out of it, at least in the frontier settings where returning as a family is hardest. Piper's fuller answer adds a mechanism that belongs beside this one, though it explains the surplus of women rather than the deficit of men: because "the initiative of proposing marriage among those two groups, singles, lies with the men and not the women," single women are disproportionately single not by choice, and so form a standing pool available to redirect toward the field (Piper 2016). It is the one explanation in this literature that accounts for why the women are available rather than why the men are absent.

(5) Discipleship failure. Akin (2022) points to spiritual immaturity among young men (pornography, laziness, a want of self-control); Jenney (2023) cites the figure that 77 percent of Christian men aged eighteen to thirty view pornography at least monthly. On the ABWE platform this hardens into a civilizational claim about "the overall downgrade of teaching on biblical manhood" (ABWE International 2019). A "courage deficit" wording sometimes hung on Piper does not hold up against his text and should not be cited.

(6) Parental discouragement. Barna research done for the IMB found that "only 35 percent of engaged Christian parents of young adults say they would definitely encourage their child to serve in missions," and that nearly half would rather their child "get a well-paying job than be a career missionary" (Barna Group 2020, as reported in Medina 2025 and Erlacher and White 2022; Jenney 2023 concurs from agency experience).

(7) Career framing. Young men hear missions pitched as a short-term experience, not a life's calling (Jenney 2023).

One caution closes the section. All seven explanations assume the deficit starts with male aspiration, and that assumption has never been tested. A statistic that surfaces now and then in mobilization discussions (that actively churched young men express more interest in a missions career than young women) could not be traced to any primary source during this study's verification, and is set aside here. The question therefore stays open. Does the shortage begin in aspiration itself, or in the pipeline between aspiration and deployment, where debt, support raising, parental pressure, and the pastoral-track alternative all operate? It is an empirical question, and among the most consequential this literature leaves unanswered (§11).

6. What the Imbalance Costs Single Women

The literature insists, almost with one voice, that the trouble is not the presence of women but the absence of men, echoing Jenney's "We don't need fewer women; we need more men!" (Jenney 2023). Yet the same practitioner writing records specific burdens the imbalance lays on the women who make up the majority of the field. Those burdens are worth naming plainly.

Interview reporting by Shirley Ralston, written for the Upstream Collective and Outreach Magazine, sets down a working hierarchy of influence on mission teams: in one interviewee's ordering, "married men, single men, married women, single women, and then, in many places, single women of color," followed by the plain testimony, "I felt like I was an afterthought" (Ralston 2021). Sarita Hartz's informal survey of roughly sixty single women missionaries found that "being single in missions presents unique challenges including safety issues, suffering, loneliness, sexism... higher levels of burnout, and grieving the diminishing possibility of marriage," and that these women felt treated differently from single men, the men freer to act, the women's callings "diminished as fanciful or less valuable until married" or fenced off on safety grounds (Hartz 2024). The grief over marriage prospects is not soft complaint but structural arithmetic, given the ratio, and it turns up in agency-published testimony too (TEAM 2017). These are practitioner sources, interview journalism and an informal survey, not agency statistics. They are also the only systematic treatments to be found, and their agreement with agency-published anecdote gives them weight.

Two more consequences bear on strategy rather than welfare. Take attrition first. OMF's singles research marks age forty as a turning point, the age at which many single women make hard decisions about their future in missions (OMF International n.d.-b). My own Central Asian observation adds an earlier threshold (§3.5): among single women, attrition looks concentrated at the third year abroad, and those who last past it tend to outstay the average, while single men leave instead through marriage and non-return. If that pattern holds more widely, member care for single women should be loaded into the first three years, and the retention question for single men is less "how do we keep them single" than "how do we make return-with-a-family viable." Then there is the matter of witness. Beyond Jenney's question of what a two-thirds-female force says about the faith, the AIM reporting noted that single women have "taken up the work, sometimes doing jobs that a male presence would be better suited to" (Mission Network News 2013). That is a claim about role fit in patriarchal host cultures, and it recurs in gentler form in agency discussions of reaching secluded women (Parrish 2018; Wade 2021), where the very social segregation that makes women essential for reaching women makes men necessary for reaching men.

7. The Local Church as the Locus of Responsibility

The steadiest note in the prescriptive literature is a handoff of responsibility from agency to congregation. Akin's wording is the most quoted in the corpus: "I'm convinced the lack of young men on the mission field is first and foremost a local church issue.... Older men in the church can purposefully disciple young men with the nations in mind... show the next generation that God is a missionary God and the Bible is a missionary book" (Akin 2022). Buser puts the turnaround "with the church and with parents," and faults pulpits that never set the frontier before their young men (Buser 2019). Jenney's remedies are congregational too: deal with the heart issues, disciple young men toward truth about provision, sexuality, and vocation, and press them to consider going (Jenney 2023). From the other side, SIM's Kuntenga asks churches to quit treating marriage as a precondition for being useful in ministry (SIM 2025). Ralston's prescription for churches that send single women is a four-part discipline: respect her as an adult, set clear expectations, help her guard against loneliness, and "keep the lifelines firmly in place while she serves, and when she returns home" (Ralston 2021).

The convergence is no theological surprise, since evangelical missiology has leaned toward church-centered sending for a generation. But applied to the gender question it carries a sharper edge. On the pastoral-track account (§5), the local church is not just the neglected cure but part of the cause, because it is congregational culture that steers its most zealous men toward the home pulpit and its most zealous women toward the field.

8. Professional Pathways and the Question of Men

A separate and older literature commends professional, "tentmaking," and marketplace pathways as strategic access to countries closed to ordinary missionaries. Its foundations are three. Ruth Siemens's Pauline argument that "The mission community is mainly interested in professionals for creative access to that 70% to 80% of the world which restricts the entry of missionaries" (Siemens 1998). J. Christy Wilson's Today's Tentmakers (1979). And the Lausanne movement's 2004 Business as Mission consultation, whose manifesto calls on the church worldwide "to identify, affirm, pray for, commission and release business people and entrepreneurs to exercise their gifts and calling as business people in the world – among all peoples and to the ends of the earth" (Lausanne Movement 2004). The present-day institutional forms follow suit. They include Crossworld's marketplace model ("disciple-makers from all professions"), OM's Scatter Global, Interserve's professional partnerships, the IMB's Global Cities Initiative with its "access consultants" for business professionals (Baptist Press 2016), and a crowded field of newer mobilizers whose slogan-register one 2026 formulation catches well: "The door is closed to the missionary title. But it remains wide open to the engineer, the educator, the healthcare professional, and the entrepreneur" (Go Live Serve 2026).

This study went looking specifically for a stated connection between that literature and the male deficit, the hypothesis that professional pathways, by keeping career identity, income, and provider capacity intact, would recruit the very men whom support-raised missions loses. The finding is negative, and instructive for being so: no agency or mobilization source states the connection explicitly. The nearest approach comes from the field side rather than the sending side: one practitioner essay records men in home-country ministry volunteering the route themselves—willing, they said, to go overseas and work a paying job, but finding the thought of asking churches for support "arduous and humiliating" (A Life Overseas 2022). The syllogism builds itself from premises lying right next to each other. The male-specific barriers named in §5 (career sacrifice, provision anxiety, an aversion to raising support) are exactly the frictions marketplace models advertise themselves as removing, and the agency that publishes most doggedly about missing men (Crossworld) is also the agency whose whole model is the marketplace. Even so, the synthesis appears nowhere in print I could reach. A researcher or church leader may put it forward as a hypothesis; none may cite it as an agency claim. Two cautions follow. First, marketplace models do not remove the support barrier outright: Crossworld, for one, runs a hybrid funding model in which even salaried marketplace workers raise partial support (Crossworld n.d.). Second, no outcome data exist on whether professional-pathway programs actually enroll men at higher rates than traditional tracks. The question is obvious and researchable, and unanswered.

9. Initiatives in Operation

Concrete programs against the named barriers do exist, though almost none is cast in gendered terms. Take debt first. The GO Fund (founded c. 2012) takes over the student-loan payments of missionaries serving among the unreached, in formal partnership with Radius International and ABWE (The GO Fund n.d.; Adams 2020), and MedSend (1992) has made educational-debt grants to more than seven hundred healthcare missionaries (MedSend n.d.). Then the pipeline. The Upstream Collective builds church-based sending pipelines. Radius International runs nine-month residential pre-field training for "singles, couples, and families... committed to long-term, pioneer church planting among unreached language groups," and requires local-church approval of every candidate (Radius International n.d.). The Southern Baptist system funds pathways from seminary to field, among them the thirty-year-old "2+2" programs, full IMB-missionary scholarships at Southeastern Seminary, the sixty-year-old Journeyman program for ages twenty-one to twenty-nine, and a new IMB–Send Network church-planting partnership (Alabama Baptist 2025; IMB 2025). Sector-wide numbers give the scale. Missio Nexus's 2025 mobilization report logs 14,544 expressions of long-term interest yielding 1,051 deployed long-term workers across roughly seventy-six agencies, a conversion rate near seven percent (Missio Nexus 2025).

Programming aimed squarely at men is nearly nonexistent. The one located example is Men for Missions, founded in 1954 within One Mission Society "because he [Dwight Ferguson] saw a severe lack of men serving on the mission field" (One Mission Society n.d.), and it mobilizes lay teams rather than long-term senders. Women-targeted structures, by contrast, are common (SEND's Women's Ministry, Wycliffe's Women of the Word, the IMB's single-women content vertical). Seventy years after Ferguson, the "more men" conviction lives in essays, podcasts, and pulpit appeals. It has not yet produced a program.

10. Discussion: Implications for Humanitarian-Oriented Churches and Denominations

Take a conservative evangelical denomination whose overseas work now leans toward relief and development rather than frontier church planting. For a body like that, the findings gather into six theses.

First, the imbalance hides on the humanitarian side and cannot be dodged on the frontier side. Relief postings in accessible countries tend to be shorter, they run through institutions, and they lean less on the lone long-term worker; the shortage traced here bunches up "especially in the most challenging places" (Wade 2021). Reorient toward unreached peoples and the imbalance stops being a curiosity. It becomes a planning constraint of the first order.

Second, the sending church gets named as both cause and cure, and named by the agencies themselves. Most of the barriers set out in §5 sit on ground the congregation controls: parental formation, debt counsel, support-raising culture, vocational imagination, and the routing of gifted men.

Third, complementarian bodies in particular owe candid attention to the pastoral-track siphon. Reserve the pulpit for men, then hold up the pastorate as the highest calling for your most devoted sons, and you should not be surprised to find your mission force largely female; the answering discipline is to set the frontier before gifted young men before the domestic pulpit claims them.

Fourth, professional pathways are the natural bridge for a humanitarian-oriented constituency. A denomination like this usually runs deep in exactly the vocations (medicine, engineering, education, agriculture, business) that restricted countries let in and that the Lausanne manifesto asks churches to "commission and release" (Lausanne Movement 2004). Whether these same pathways will pull in men at higher rates is a further hypothesis. It is plausible, but, as §8 showed, one I have to own as inference rather than finding.

Fifth, raising up men must never be bought at the price of belittling the women already on the field. Here the corpus does not disagree with itself. The practitioner literature (§6) even spells out what a church owes the single women it sends, namely respect, plain dealing, companionship, and lifelines that hold through service and the return home. The wider movement has even produced guard-rails around the slogan this article takes for its title: Thabiti Anyabwile, reacting to "we need more men" conference rhetoric as early as 2011, warned complementarians against treating their sisters as "'second string' players" (Anyabwile 2011), and The Gospel Coalition later ran the counterweight to its own Akin piece—Elliot Clark's argument that the inverse does not follow: "we don't need fewer women serving cross-culturally," and "even if they continue to outnumber men, there's still room for more" (Clark 2023).

Sixth, cite the numbers honestly. What holds up is the range, not a single headline figure. The defensible numbers are IMB's official 2:1, the survey's 74 percent of singles, and 4:1 as the conventional high-end figure, that last one resting on "widespread anecdotal data" (Akin 2022). Let the direction, the persistence, and the frontier concentration of the imbalance carry the weight. Even the conservative figures make the case; overstate it and you simply hand critics a target.

11. Limitations and Further Research

This study reaches only as far as publicly indexed English-language sources. Internal agency data, member-gated Missio Nexus reports, and the talk at conferences all lie outside it. I have re-checked the quotations against live pages, archived snapshots, or primary documents; the caveats that remain sit in the notes.¹ My own participant observation in §3.5 has its own limits, and I want to be plain about them. It rests on one worker's decade in a single region, my own. The counts are unaudited, leaning partly on memory and partly on one director's informal report. And Central Asia, strongly Islamic and hard to enter, may be a poor stand-in for the mission field as a whole, which is exactly why I offer it, as a probe of the frontier end of the distribution rather than its mean.

Five research directions follow straight from this. (1) Roster-level measurement: agencies hold the sex and age composition of their forces but do not publish it; one confidential multi-agency census would replace a century of estimates. (2) The aspiration-to-deployment pipeline: I could locate no reliable published measure of missions interest by gender among young adults (a widely repeated figure to that effect failed verification), and establishing that baseline, then tracking over time where male candidates exit, would test every explanation in §5. (3) Pathway effects: whether marketplace and professional programs enroll men at higher rates than support-raised tracks is measurable, and no one has measured it. (4) The young-couples question: no direct data exist, though agency application records since 2017 could settle it. (5) Attrition structure by sex and marital status: the bimodal pattern observed in §3.5 and §6 (a third-year threshold for single women, marriage-and-non-return for single men) can be tested against agency personnel records, and confirming it would sharpen member-care design considerably.

12. Conclusion

Zwemer's 1911 lament has hardened into a settled demographic structure: a missionary force two-thirds female overall, with single women outnumbering single men by anywhere from two- to more-than-four-to-one (and, in my own decade of observation among Central Asian Muslim peoples, nearer five-to-one), worst of all at the very frontiers evangelicals claim as their priority. The agencies' own literature lands on a diagnosis that is congregational rather than organizational. Young men are not being formed, freed, or asked. Its prescription hands the question back to the local church, the same church the literature blames for the causes. In essays the movement has said a great deal; in programs it has said little. What exists goes after debt and the pipeline in general, and the one squarely male-targeted organization dates from 1954. Meanwhile the women who make up the field's majority carry documented costs (hierarchy, restriction, loneliness, and structural grief) that sending churches are only beginning to name. So the composition of the sending body is not a staffing detail. For any church weighing a turn from humanitarian engagement toward the unreached, it is a strategic and pastoral variable of the first order, and the honest statistics, softer than the slogans though they are, are still more than enough to warrant the attention they have not yet received.
